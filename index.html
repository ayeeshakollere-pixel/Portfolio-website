The Question I Asked

Is Shell getting their money's worth in emissions reductions for every dollar spent on green transition?

By connecting five years of raw financial capital records with environmental data, the numbers tell a clear story about transition economics—and reveal where capital actually works.

What's Actually Happening

Shell's green spending jumped from $1.1 Billion (15% of total CapEx) in 2021 to $4.8 Billion (28%) in 2025. That's massive acceleration into renewables and clean infrastructure.

But here's the real story: In 2022, Shell got 21,905 tonnes of CO2 removed for every $1 million invested. By 2025, that dropped to 13,438 tonnes per million dollars. Same money, 38% fewer emissions removed. The efficiency is declining.

Why This Matters

Early wins were easy. First solar projects, first wind farms—high payback, massive carbon reduction. But scaling hits real constraints. Bigger projects take longer. Grid upgrades cost more. Deep decarbonization requires infrastructure that's harder to build.

The implication: If Shell keeps spending $4.8B annually but efficiency stays flat at 13,438 tCO2e/$M, they hit a math problem: they'll need accelerating capital just to maintain emissions reductions, never mind reaching net-zero. Or they need ruthless prioritization—investing only in the highest-efficiency projects and deprioritizing everything else.

What I Investigated

Lending Club is a peer-to-peer lending platform. They've originated $25+ billion in loans since 2007. But not all loans get repaid. Some people default. The question: Can I predict who will default before they get the loan?

The Data Science Part

I pulled 250,000+ loan records from BigQuery (a Google database tool). For each loan, I looked at: credit score, income, loan amount, employment history, how many accounts the person has open, and whether they've been late before.

Then I built a statistical model to see which factors actually predict default. I used cohort analysis—grouping people by similar characteristics and watching how many defaulted in each group.

What I found matters: People with lower credit scores default more (obvious but measurable). People who have been late on payments before are 3x more likely to default again. People with higher debt-to-income ratios are riskier. Interestingly, loan amount matters less than people think—a person's payment history matters way more.

Why This Matters in Real Life

Lending Club could use this to make better decisions: approve people who look risky but have strong income and clean payment history. Deny people with low credit scores and spotty payment history, even if they have high income. This isn't about being mean—it's about reducing losses and making smarter bets.

What I learned: Data isn't just numbers in a spreadsheet. It's about understanding real behavior. A person's past is the best predictor of their future. And sometimes what looks obvious (people with low income default more) is actually less predictive than what's subtle (people who make late payments are chronically unreliable).

Understanding how a private company scales when there's no public earnings report

What I Actually Did Here

Canonical doesn't file quarterly earnings. No investor decks. No earnings calls. So I rebuilt their story from three pieces of public data that everyone can see — but most people miss.

The three signals that matter

Developers pick Ubuntu. 27.8% of software developers use it. That's the #1 Linux choice globally. Developers choose tools based on what works — not marketing. When developers love something, organizations eventually buy it to support them. That's the funnel.

It's everywhere in the cloud. More than 60% of public cloud servers run Ubuntu. Google Cloud, AWS, Azure — when you spin up a new server, Ubuntu is the default. That's not accident. That's market dominance. 67,000 new instances launch daily on Ubuntu. That's real scale.

Organizations actually pay for it. Canonical earned $292M in 2024. That's real revenue from real customers buying support. The gross margin is 83% — which means once Canonical builds the product, selling it costs almost nothing. That's the economics of infrastructure software.

What's actually impressive

They scaled from $205M (2022) to $292M (2024). Revenue growing 19% year-over-year is solid. But here's what makes it real: they're doing it profitably. They're not burning cash to grab market share. They're building a business that actually works.

They hired more people (headcount up 37%) to support their growth. That's smart. More customers need more support. More cloud instances need more engineering behind them. They're scaling thoughtfully.

The real story: A free, open-source product that developers love. It becomes the default in cloud. Then the company monetizes with premium support for enterprises that need it. That's the ideal playbook for infrastructure software. And Canonical is executing it.
